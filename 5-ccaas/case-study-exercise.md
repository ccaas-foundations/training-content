## Slide 1 - The situation

Answer these three questions:

**1. What was the problem?**
Describe the business problem the company was trying to solve - not the technology problem. What was failing, slow, expensive, or limited? Who was affected and how?

**2. What was the solution?**
What did they build or implement? Name the specific technologies involved. If the article names a platform, a service, or an integration, call it out explicitly.

**3. What curriculum concepts do you recognize?**
Look at the technologies and processes described and map them to what we covered this week. You should be able to identify at least two. Examples: IVR, ACD, CCaaS, CRM integration, omnichannel routing, screen pop, on-premise vs. cloud, AI-native contact center, containment rate, after-call work.

---

## Slide 2 - The analysis

**4. What were the outcomes?**
What specific metrics or results did they cite? Be precise - numbers matter. If no numbers are given, note that and explain what you think should have been measured.

**5. What is this article not telling you?**
Every vendor case study is written to make the vendor look good. What questions does it leave unanswered? What risks, tradeoffs, or challenges are glossed over or absent entirely? Think about: What did the migration actually cost? How long did it take? What broke along the way? What does the solution still not do? What would you want to know before recommending this approach to a client?

**6. If you were the developer on this engagement, what would your role have been?**
Based on the integration work described - CRM connections, API calls, IVR flows, analytics pipelines - what would a developer actually have built? Be specific about the technical work implied even if the article doesn't describe it directly.

## A note on reading case studies critically

These articles are published by AWS. They are marketing material. That does not mean they are wrong - the outcomes described are real - but it means they are selectively presented. The problems are framed to make the solution look inevitable. The metrics cited are the ones that improved. The things that were hard, slow, or expensive are not in the article.

In client-facing work you will read a lot of material like this - vendor case studies, whitepapers, solution briefs. Practice extracting what is genuinely useful while asking the questions the document does not answer.
